# Review: Fiona Fidler (metascience, elicitation protocols)

*Reviewing `claim-dag-audit` against the question: is this ready to run its first pilot (the Kinds paper) as designed? My lens is protocol design and measurement: extraction/judgment reliability, calibration, and outcome feedback.*

## 1. One-sentence summary

`claim-dag-audit` is a file-first CLI that re-represents a paper as a directed graph of claim nodes and inference edges, then audits nodes (truth, source grounding) and edges (does the support hold) separately, with a final adversarial pass, treating a node or edge as `cleared` only when a deliberate attack was tried and failed.

## 2. Strengths

- **The node/edge split is the right measurement move.** Separating "is this claim true / source-grounded" (node audit) from "do these premises actually license this conclusion" (edge audit) is exactly the decomposition that lets you locate *where* an argument fails instead of scoring a whole paper as one gestalt. The audit prompts hold the line: `audit-edge.md` step 1 forces "assume the source claims are true," which isolates the inference from the truth of its inputs. That is a genuine analytic contribution, not just tooling.
- **`cleared` is defined by process, not by plausibility, and the team knows it.** README.md:86-87, DECISIONS.md (2026-07-05 entry), and CLAUDE.md all repeat that `cleared` means an attack failed, not that the prose read well. Metascience spends a lot of energy fighting exactly the slippage from "sounds convincing" to "is warranted"; the project has that instinct in the right place from the start.
- **File-first, diffable, plain YAML/Markdown.** For a method whose whole promise is inspectability, storing state as version-controlled YAML rather than a database is the correct call (DECISIONS.md). It means the audit record travels with the paper and every change is, in principle, reconstructable from git.

## 3. Weaknesses

Three, in ascending order of how much they threaten the pilot.

**(a) The status vocabulary is two conflated coding schemes with no codebook, so it cannot be coded reliably.** `CLAIM_STATUSES` in `claim_dag/schema.py:15-23` mixes *workflow* states (`needs-audit`, `in-progress`, `deferred`) with *audit-outcome* states (`cleared`, `weakened`, `failed`), and `uncleared` straddles both. In the example (`examples/mini-paper/audits/claim-dag/2026-07-05/claims.yaml`) the two definitional/stipulative claims are `uncleared` while the inference is `needs-audit`, with nothing anywhere stating the rule that distinguishes those two "not yet audited" labels. Meanwhile `schema.py:26` sets `EDGE_STATUSES = CLAIM_STATUSES` (7 values), but `schemas/audit.schema.yaml:3,6` lists only 4 verdicts (`cleared, weakened, failed, deferred`). So there are two status vocabularies of different arity, and the mapping from an audit verdict to the status written back onto the node/edge is specified nowhere. No status has a prose definition, a positive example, or a boundary case anywhere in the repo. Where is the line between `weakened` and `failed`? Between `uncleared` and `needs-audit`? Two auditors will not agree, and you have no way to measure that they didn't. *Actionable:* write a one-page codebook: one sentence definition + one in-scope example + one boundary example per status; collapse `uncleared`/`needs-audit` unless you can state the rule; separate the workflow axis from the outcome axis into two fields.

**(b) `cleared` is unfalsifiable in the data model: nothing links a status to its audit, and no outcome can ever return to show it wrong.** This is the calibration problem, and it is the one I care about most. `schemas/audit.schema.yaml:4-6` requires `edge_id, verdict, auditor, date, suppressed_premise, attack` in an audit file, which is good, but `validate_edges` (`claim_dag/schema.py:87-128`) never opens `node-audits/` or `edge-audits/`. I confirmed this: I copied the example, set every claim and the edge to `status: cleared` with **no audit files present at all**, and `claim-dag validate` exited 0 with zero errors. So `cleared` is a bare, hand-editable scalar with no required evidentiary trace. Worse, there is no history, no re-audit trigger, no versioning of the paper the anchors point to, and `io.write_yaml` (`claim_dag/io.py:17-20`) overwrites status in place. A later, stronger attack that breaks a "cleared" edge has nowhere to be recorded and nothing it updates; the tool can never surface "this cleared verdict was wrong." A verdict system with no error-detecting feedback is precisely the machinery metascience warns manufactures false assurance. *Actionable:* make `validate` refuse `status: cleared` on any node/edge that lacks a matching audit file whose verdict is `cleared`; give each status an append-only history (auditor, date, verdict, attack-ref) instead of a scalar; store a hash of the source span so anchors rot loudly, not silently.

**(c) Extraction and edge-drawing are unconstrained judgment with no reliability procedure, and the format has nowhere to hold the process data a pilot exists to collect.** `prompts/extract-claims.md` gives no unit-of-analysis rule ("split compound statements when the parts could fail independently" is itself a judgment call), and `anchor` is "verbatim **or near-verbatim**" (line 8) which is neither a checkable span nor a paraphrase and cannot be diffed between auditors. `prompts/build-edges.md` and the schema enumerate four relations (`supports, requires, rebuts, qualifies`) but define none, though the `supports`/`requires` distinction is clearly meant to carry weight. Two auditors handed the Kinds spine will produce different claim counts, different type assignments, and different edge sets, and the design gives you no way to quantify the gap. Relatedly, the pilot most needs process telemetry to evaluate the *method*: time per claim/edge, status transitions and churn, inter-auditor disagreement, and a **confidence/credence per verdict**. The format holds almost none of it: `claims.yaml`/`edges.yaml` carry a single scalar `status` with no timestamp, auditor, effort, or history (`schemas/claims.schema.yaml`, `schemas/edges.schema.yaml`); `audit.schema.yaml` is single-`auditor` by construction with no time-spent, round number, or second-coder slot; and there is no `confidence` field anywhere, so a 4-valued verdict could never be scored (no Brier-style calibration is even possible on a bare ordinal). *Actionable:* for the pilot, have two people independently extract claims and edges on the same spine and compute crude agreement (claim count, type-agreement rate, edge-set Jaccard, disagreement log); add fields for auditor, elapsed time, a numeric confidence, and an audit-file reference; require verbatim spans with offsets, not "near-verbatim."

The code observations above I checked directly against the files and by running `validate`.)*

## 4. Key question

Before you run the Kinds pilot: what is the outcome signal against which a `cleared` verdict could later be scored as correct or incorrect, and where in these files does that signal get recorded? If the honest answer is "there isn't one yet," then run the very first pilot as a **two-auditor reliability study** (independent extraction + independent edge audits on the same spine, agreement computed, disagreements logged) rather than a single-coder demonstration, because otherwise the pilot teaches you what the graph looks like but nothing about whether the method is reliable or its `cleared` labels mean anything.

## 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The decomposition is sound and the file-first design is right, but as built the pilot is single-coder with no reliability measurement, no confidence elicitation, an unbacked and unfalsifiable `cleared` status (validation passes with `cleared` and zero audit files), and status categories that lack a codebook; add a two-auditor agreement pass, a confidence field, and a validation rule tying `cleared` to a passing audit record before running it.
